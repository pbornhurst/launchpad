# Restaurant Operations

---

### OPS-001 Order Flow From Ticket to Table
- **Difficulty:** beginner
- **Durations:** 1 min, 3 min
- **Core thesis:** Understanding the journey of a single order through a restaurant reveals why operations technology matters — every step is a potential failure point.
- **Key talking points:**
  1. The order lifecycle: customer places order → POS captures it → ticket hits kitchen → food is prepared → order is fulfilled (dine-in, pickup, or delivery)
  2. Where things break: wrong items, missed modifiers, slow ticket times, lost orders
  3. The role of KDS (Kitchen Display Systems) in replacing paper tickets
  4. How multi-channel orders (in-store + online + delivery) create chaos without proper routing
- **Real-world hooks:** Restaurants running DoorDash tablets alongside their POS, manually re-entering orders. Pathfinder's integrated approach eliminating double-entry. The "ticket printer ran out of paper" disaster.
- **Landmine to avoid:** Don't oversimplify — restaurant workflows vary wildly by concept (fast casual vs full service vs QSR).

### OPS-002 Why Restaurants Fail in the First Year
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Durations:** 3 min, 5 min
- **Core thesis:** The "90% of restaurants fail" stat is a myth, but the real failure rate (around 60% in 5 years) is still brutal — and the causes are more about operations and cash flow than bad food.
- **Key talking points:**
  1. The real stats: ~17% fail in year one, ~60% by year five (cite National Restaurant Association data)
  2. Top killers: undercapitalization, poor location, labor costs, food cost mismanagement
  3. Why food quality is rarely the problem — most failed restaurants served decent food
  4. The role of technology (or lack of it): restaurants flying blind without data on their own operations
  5. What survivors do differently: obsessive cost control, menu engineering, and using data to make decisions
- **Real-world hooks:** Restaurants on Pathfinder discovering their actual food costs for the first time through reporting. The mx who didn't know 40% of their orders were coming through delivery until they saw the data.
- **Landmine to avoid:** Don't be preachy about tech solving everything — many successful restaurants use minimal technology. The point is about operational awareness, not specific tools.

### OPS-003 Kitchen Display Systems and Their Impact
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Durations:** 1 min, 3 min, 5 min
- **Core thesis:** KDS is the most underrated piece of restaurant tech — it quietly reduces errors, speeds up ticket times, and gives operators visibility they never had with paper tickets.
- **Key talking points:**
  1. Paper tickets vs digital displays: readability, routing, priority management, analytics
  2. How KDS reduces errors: color-coded modifiers, allergy alerts, order consolidation
  3. The data layer: average ticket times, station bottleneck identification, prep time tracking
  4. Multi-channel routing: sending in-store orders to one screen, delivery orders to another
- **Real-world hooks:** Pathfinder's integrated KDS. Restaurants that cut ticket times by 20%+ after switching from paper. The kitchen that discovered their grill station was the bottleneck, not the fryer.
- **Landmine to avoid:** Don't ignore the downsides — KDS screens break, they need reliable WiFi, and kitchen staff need training to adopt them.

### OPS-004 The Labor Squeeze and How Tech Responds
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Durations:** 3 min, 5 min
- **Core thesis:** The restaurant labor shortage isn't temporary — it's structural, and the industry's response is a mix of automation, better scheduling, and rethinking the service model entirely.
- **Key talking points:**
  1. The numbers: restaurant industry has 500K+ unfilled positions, turnover exceeds 70% annually
  2. Why it's structural: wages in competing industries (warehouses, gig economy), demanding hours, limited benefits
  3. Tech responses: self-service kiosks, QR code ordering, automated scheduling, robotic kitchen assistants
  4. The service model shift: fast casual replacing full service, counter service replacing table service
  5. The tension: automation can feel impersonal, and hospitality is fundamentally human
- **Real-world hooks:** Kiosk adoption at Pathfinder mx — reducing the need for a dedicated cashier. QR code ordering post-COVID staying permanent. The Sweetgreen Infinite Kitchen. Restaurants running skeleton crews with tech filling the gaps.
- **Landmine to avoid:** Don't frame automation as "replacing workers" — frame it as "augmenting a workforce that doesn't exist." Operators aren't choosing robots over people; they can't find people.
