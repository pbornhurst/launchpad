### IND-001 The Rise of Ghost Kitchens

- **Difficulty:** beginner
- **Durations:** 1 min, 3 min
- **Core thesis:** Ghost kitchens were supposed to revolutionize the restaurant industry by separating cooking from real estate — the reality turned out to be more complicated, but the concept permanently changed how operators think about space.
- **Key talking points:**
  1. What a ghost kitchen is: production-only facility with no dine-in, built for delivery
  2. The COVID boom: how pandemic shutdowns accelerated the model by 5-10 years
  3. The correction: CloudKitchens, Kitchen United, and others scaling back after over-expansion
  4. What survived: virtual brands (multiple concepts from one kitchen) and hybrid models
- **Real-world hooks:** DoorDash's virtual restaurant program. Restaurants that launched delivery-only brands from their existing kitchen. The Wow Bao model (licensing a brand to existing kitchens).
- **Landmine to avoid:** Don't declare ghost kitchens dead or alive — the truth is nuanced. Some models work, some don't. Focus on what operators can learn from the experiment.

### IND-002 AI in Restaurants — Hype vs Reality

- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Durations:** 3 min, 5 min
- **Core thesis:** AI is making real inroads in restaurants in narrow, specific applications (demand forecasting, voice ordering, dynamic pricing), but the industry is years away from the "AI-powered restaurant" vision that vendors are selling.
- **Key talking points:**
  1. What's working now: demand forecasting for prep, automated phone answering, dynamic menu pricing, food waste prediction
  2. What's overhyped: fully autonomous kitchens, AI replacing managers, chatbot ordering that actually works well
  3. The data problem: most restaurants don't have clean, structured data to train models on
  4. The adoption gap: restaurant operators are hands-on people, not data scientists — AI needs to be invisible to work
  5. Where it's heading: AI as copilot (suggestions, automations) not as replacement
- **Real-world hooks:** DoorDash using AI/ML for delivery time estimates and dasher routing. Presto's voice AI for drive-throughs. The gap between what AI demos show and what actually works in a busy kitchen at 7pm on a Friday.
- **Landmine to avoid:** Don't be a tech bro about this — restaurant operators have heard every "this will change everything" pitch. Be grounded in what actually helps them make money or save time today.

### IND-003 Why QR Code Ordering Stuck Around

- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Durations:** 1 min, 3 min
- **Core thesis:** QR code ordering was a pandemic necessity that became a permanent fixture — not because consumers love it, but because the economics of reduced labor are too compelling for operators to abandon.
- **Key talking points:**
  1. Pre-COVID: QR codes were considered dead technology in the US
  2. The pandemic pivot: contactless ordering became a health requirement overnight
  3. Why it stuck: labor savings (fewer servers needed), higher average check (upsell prompts), faster table turns
  4. The consumer pushback: many diners hate it — it feels impersonal, phones die, older customers struggle
- **Real-world hooks:** Full-service restaurants that cut front-of-house staff by 30% with QR ordering. The backlash: restaurants advertising "we still have real menus" as a differentiator. Asia's years-ahead adoption (WeChat ordering in China).
- **Landmine to avoid:** Don't take a strong position for or against — the interesting story is the tension between operator economics and consumer preference.

### IND-004 Restaurant Tech Consolidation

- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Durations:** 3 min, 5 min
- **Core thesis:** The restaurant tech landscape is consolidating rapidly — the era of 50 point solutions is ending, replaced by a few large platforms that want to own the entire stack, and this has massive implications for operators.
- **Key talking points:**
  1. The fragmented past: separate vendors for POS, online ordering, delivery, loyalty, scheduling, inventory, payments
  2. The consolidation wave: Toast acquiring multiple companies, DoorDash building Pathfinder, Square expanding into restaurants
  3. Why it's happening: operators want fewer vendors, investors want platform economics, data advantages compound
  4. The risk for operators: lock-in, reduced negotiating power, "good enough" replacing "best in class"
  5. Who wins: platforms that solve the most painful problems first and build trust before expanding
- **Real-world hooks:** Toast's IPO and acquisition spree. DoorDash Commerce Platform strategy. The independent POS companies getting squeezed or acquired. Restaurant operators who consolidated from 8 vendors to 2.
- **Landmine to avoid:** Don't make it sound inevitable that one player wins everything — restaurants are diverse, and the winner in QSR might be different from the winner in fine dining.
