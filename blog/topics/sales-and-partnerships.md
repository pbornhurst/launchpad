### SAL-001 Pitching POS to a Skeptical Restaurant Owner
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Durations:** 3 min, 5 min
- **Core thesis:** Restaurant owners have been burned by tech vendors before — winning their trust requires leading with their pain points, not your product features, and being honest about trade-offs.
- **Key talking points:**
  1. Why they're skeptical: bad past experiences, high switching costs, "we've heard this before" fatigue
  2. The discovery approach: ask about their biggest operational headache before mentioning your product
  3. Common pain points that open doors: manual reconciliation, lack of delivery/in-store reporting, card reader frustration with current POS
  4. The proof points that matter: "restaurants like yours" stories, specific dollar amounts saved, time saved per week
  5. What not to do: feature-dump, trash their current system, promise things you can't deliver
- **Real-world hooks:** The mx who switched after their legacy POS lost 3 days of sales data. The pitch that worked by focusing on reporting, not the POS itself. Owner objections: "I've been using [legacy system] for 15 years."
- **Landmine to avoid:** Don't come across as selling — this is about consultative problem-solving. The audience for this talk should feel like you're sharing real lessons, not rehearsing a pitch deck.

### SAL-002 The QBR as a Relationship Tool
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Durations:** 1 min, 3 min, 5 min
- **Core thesis:** A Quarterly Business Review isn't a reporting exercise — it's the single best tool for deepening the merchant relationship, surfacing unspoken problems, and positioning yourself as an indispensable partner.
- **Key talking points:**
  1. What most people get wrong: treating QBRs as a data dump instead of a conversation
  2. The structure that works: 20% looking back (data review), 80% looking forward (strategy, opportunities)
  3. The data that matters: trends over time (not snapshots), benchmarks vs similar restaurants, actionable insights
  4. The relationship payoff: QBRs surface issues before they become churn risks and create expansion opportunities
  5. The tactical details: send data ahead of time, keep it under 30 minutes, always end with clear next steps
- **Real-world hooks:** The QBR that saved a churning mx because they finally felt heard. The mx who expanded to 3 more locations after a QBR showed their growth potential. Using MSAT scores to gauge relationship health before the meeting.
- **Landmine to avoid:** Don't make QBRs sound formulaic — every restaurant is different, and the best QBRs feel like genuine conversations, not scripted presentations.

### SAL-003 How to Talk About Volume Drops Without Losing Trust
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Durations:** 3 min, 5 min
- **Core thesis:** When a merchant's volume drops, they already know something is wrong — the AM's job isn't to deliver bad news, it's to show you've already been paying attention and have a plan to help.
- **Key talking points:**
  1. The wrong approach: "We noticed your volume is down" (accusatory, implies you only call when numbers drop)
  2. The right approach: "I've been looking at your data and I have some ideas" (proactive, solution-oriented)
  3. Diagnosing the cause: seasonal? operational change? competitor opened nearby? card reader issues? they switched to another POS?
  4. The conversation structure: acknowledge the situation, share what you've found, propose specific next steps, commit to follow-up
  5. When volume drops mean churn: recognizing the difference between a dip and a trend, and when to escalate internally
- **Real-world hooks:** The mx who went dark because of a broken card reader nobody reported. The seasonal pattern that looked like churn but wasn't. The mx who was testing a competitor and came back after a proactive call.
- **Landmine to avoid:** Don't pretend you have all the answers — sometimes volume drops for reasons you can't see from the data. Acknowledge what you don't know and ask.

### SAL-004 Onboarding as the Make-or-Break Moment
- **Difficulty:** beginner
- **Durations:** 1 min, 3 min
- **Core thesis:** The first 30 days after a restaurant signs up determine whether they become a loyal, long-term user or a quiet churn statistic — and most onboarding processes leave too much to chance.
- **Key talking points:**
  1. The reality: by the time a restaurant signs, they're excited but also nervous — the onboarding experience either validates or undermines that decision
  2. What good onboarding looks like: clear timeline, single point of contact, menu configured before install day, staff training scheduled in advance
  3. Where it breaks: delayed hardware, no follow-up after install, staff not trained, "call us if you need anything" as the onboarding strategy
  4. The metric: time from contract to first real transaction is the onboarding KPI that matters most
- **Real-world hooks:** The mx who was live in 48 hours vs the one who took 6 weeks. Pathfinder's onboarding call structure. The correlation between onboarding call completion and 90-day retention.
- **Landmine to avoid:** Don't blame the operator for slow onboarding — it's almost always a process failure on the vendor side. Own the journey.
