### DD-001 How DoorDash Marketplace Works for Merchants
- **Difficulty:** beginner
- **Durations:** 1 min, 3 min
- **Core thesis:** DoorDash's marketplace is a demand generation engine for restaurants — it puts them in front of millions of consumers they'd never reach on their own, but that access comes with trade-offs operators need to understand.
- **Key talking points:**
  1. The three-sided marketplace: consumers, merchants, dashers — and how each side's incentives interact
  2. What merchants get: incremental demand, delivery logistics, consumer app presence, marketing tools
  3. The cost structure: commission rates, how they vary by plan (Basic, Plus, Premier)
  4. The discovery algorithm: how restaurants rank in search results and why it matters
- **Real-world hooks:** Pathfinder mx who saw 30%+ of revenue come from DoorDash. The restaurant that didn't know they were on DoorDash (partner vs non-partner dynamics). How menu photos impact conversion rates.
- **Landmine to avoid:** Don't sound like a DoorDash sales pitch. Acknowledge the commission tension honestly — it's the #1 thing restaurant owners push back on.

### DD-002 First-Party vs Third-Party Ordering
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Durations:** 1 min, 3 min, 5 min
- **Core thesis:** The 1P vs 3P debate is a false binary — smart restaurants use both, with first-party for margin and loyalty and third-party for discovery and incremental volume.
- **Key talking points:**
  1. Definitions: 1P (restaurant's own ordering — website, app) vs 3P (marketplace — DoorDash, Uber Eats)
  2. The economics: 1P has lower fees but requires the restaurant to drive traffic; 3P has higher fees but provides demand
  3. The data ownership question: who owns the customer relationship?
  4. DoorDash Storefront as a bridge: 1P ordering powered by DoorDash infrastructure
  5. The portfolio approach: use 3P for acquisition, convert to 1P for retention
- **Real-world hooks:** Pathfinder mx running both Storefront and Marketplace. The restaurant that built a loyal 1P base and then reduced 3P dependency. DoorDash's Storefront product as a "white-label" solution.
- **Landmine to avoid:** Don't pick a side too strongly — both channels have a role. The nuance is in the strategy.

### DD-003 The DoorDash Commerce Platform Vision
- **Difficulty:** advanced
- **Durations:** 3 min, 5 min
- **Core thesis:** DoorDash is evolving from a delivery marketplace to a full commerce platform for local businesses — POS, payments, online ordering, marketing, and operations tools all under one roof.
- **Key talking points:**
  1. The evolution: delivery app → logistics platform → commerce platform
  2. The product suite: Marketplace, Storefront, Drive (white-label delivery), Pathfinder (POS), Ads & Promos
  3. Why vertical integration matters: controlling the full stack from in-store to delivery creates data advantages and better merchant outcomes
  4. The competitive landscape: Toast owns in-store and is moving to delivery; DoorDash owns delivery and is moving in-store
  5. What this means for restaurants: fewer vendors, more integrated data, but also more lock-in risk
- **Real-world hooks:** Pathfinder as the in-store piece of this vision. Mx who use Marketplace + Storefront + Pathfinder together. The "single pane of glass" concept for restaurant operators.
- **Landmine to avoid:** Don't oversell the vision — it's still early. Be honest about what's working and what's aspirational. Operators are skeptical of "platform" promises.

### DD-004 Why Restaurants Need Omnichannel
- **Difficulty:** intermediate
- **Durations:** 1 min, 3 min
- **Core thesis:** Consumers don't think in channels — they think in convenience. Restaurants that unify their in-store, online, and delivery experiences win; those that treat each as a silo lose.
- **Key talking points:**
  1. What omnichannel means in restaurants: consistent menu, pricing, loyalty, and experience across dine-in, takeout, delivery, and kiosk
  2. The current mess: different menus on DoorDash vs in-store, loyalty programs that don't cross channels, separate reporting
  3. Why it matters: a customer who orders delivery on Monday might dine in on Friday — and the restaurant should know that
  4. The tech requirement: you need a unified POS + online ordering + loyalty system to pull this off
- **Real-world hooks:** Pathfinder mx seeing unified reporting across in-store and delivery for the first time. OCL (Omni-Channel Loyalty) bringing loyalty to all channels. The mx who had three different menus across three platforms.
- **Landmine to avoid:** Don't make it sound easy — omnichannel is genuinely hard to implement, especially for independent restaurants with limited tech resources.
